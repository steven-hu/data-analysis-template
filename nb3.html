Our primary goal was to investigate delays, both by airport and over time. The core question we try to address is:

  • Which of the major US airports is the worst, in terms of delayed flights?

Note that since our data encompasses an entire population (domestic commercial flights in the US from 1987-2008), many avenues of statistical inference become unnecessary. There's nothing probabalistic that comes into play when we have a comprehensive view of the population.

We will look at perhaps the simplest measure of "worst" first, the mean delay time at each airport.

It seems that there's a decent spread between the lowest and highest averages, from about 8 for SLC to about 13 for ORD. However, examining the means is quite simplistic by itself. The inclusion of 0 delay flights (undelayed) in the means lacks some resolution in evaluating the delays per airport. The raw means fail to distinguish between airports with a large proportion of slightly delayed flights and a small proportion of heavily delayed flights.

In order to look more closely at the data, we'll examine two additional factors:

  • Average delay, only including flights that have been delayed (at least 1 minute)
  • Proportion of flights delayed

Next, let's examine the spread of delays at each airport. What we're interested in is how often delays of various severities occur. So, find proportions for each airport of flights delayed at least x minutes, as x goes from 1 to 60.

Note that for the following section the code wasn't originally run in this notebook, so to avoid the long runtime we've included the code but imported the output.

a = replicate(20, c())

for (n in 1:60) {
    for (i in airport) {  
        a[[i]] = c(a[[i]], sum(data$delay[data$origin == i] >= n)/length(data$delay[data$origin == i]))

props = c()
for (n in 1:60) {
    props = c(props, sum(airports$V4 >= n)/length(airports$V4))
}

The code above pulls a list of vectors, each vector representing an airport and consisting of the proportion of flights from that airport delayed more than x minutes, x ranging from 1 to 60. It also pulls overall proportions of flights delayed over x minutes, x ranging from 1 to 60.

** Logistic regression using log-odds success **¶

We are using a generalized linear model with log-odds success of proportions flights delayed as function over time. The linear probability model is not a good fit becuase probabilities are bound between 0 and 1. We used the simplest logit function: log(p/(1-p)), where p is defined as the proportion of flights delayed.

See: http://pages.uoregon.edu/aarong/teaching/G4075_Outline/node16.html
